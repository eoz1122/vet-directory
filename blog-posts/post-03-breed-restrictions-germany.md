# Breed Restrictions in Germany: The Complete Guide for Expats

Moving to Germany with a dog is usually straightforward. But if your dog falls into certain breeds, you'll need to work through one of the more complicated corners of German pet law: breed-specific legislation (*rassenspezifische Regelungen*).

Here's the honest truth: Germany does not have a single, unified national list of banned breeds. Breed restrictions are set at the **state (Bundesland) level**, and sometimes at the **city or municipality level** on top of that. What's fine in Hamburg might require extra permits in North Rhine-Westphalia, and might be outright banned in Bavaria.

This guide gives you the full picture, state by state.

---

## The Two Categories: "Banned" vs. "Restricted"

German breed legislation typically divides dogs into two categories:

**Category 1 ("Kampfhunde" / presumed dangerous):**
These breeds are presumed dangerous without needing any incident to prove it. Ownership is either banned outright or requires significant bureaucratic hurdles: permits, housing approval, liability insurance, muzzle-and-leash requirements in public, and sometimes a mandatory behavioral test (*Wesenstest*).

**Category 2 (potentially dangerous, case-by-case):**
These breeds may face additional requirements (insurance, leash mandates, or passing a behavioral test) but are not presumptively banned. With the right documentation, you can usually keep them.

---

## The Core Restricted Breeds (Appearing Across Most States)

These four breeds appear on virtually every state's restricted list. In several states, they are outright banned for new ownership:

- **American Pit Bull Terrier**
- **American Staffordshire Terrier**
- **Staffordshire Bull Terrier**
- **Bull Terrier**

Some states and cities also restrict crosses and mixes of these breeds, even if the dog looks only vaguely like one of the above.

## State-by-State Breakdown

### 🗺️ Berlin
**Restricted (require permit + insurance + leash/muzzle in public):**
American Pit Bull Terrier, American Staffordshire Terrier, Staffordshire Bull Terrier, Bull Terrier

**No outright ban**, but restricted breeds require registration with the district (*Bezirk*), proof of liability insurance (minimum €1 million), and a muzzle and leash in all public spaces.

A *Wesenstest* (behavioral assessment) may be required to get an exemption from some restrictions.

---

### 🗺️ Hamburg
**Banned (cannot be kept):**
American Pit Bull Terrier, American Staffordshire Terrier, Staffordshire Bull Terrier, Bull Terrier

Hamburg operates one of the stricter regimes in Germany. These four breeds cannot legally be acquired or kept in Hamburg. Dogs already registered before the ban may be grandfathered in with permits.

**Additionally regulated:**
Rottweiler, Doberman, Dogo Argentino, and several others may require a *Wesenstest*.

---

### 🗺️ Bavaria (München, Nürnberg, Augsburg etc.)
**Banned:**
American Pit Bull Terrier, American Staffordshire Terrier, Staffordshire Bull Terrier, Bull Terrier

Bavaria bans these four breeds. Mixed breeds that appear to have significant characteristics of these breeds may also be subject to assessment.

**No second category.** Bavaria does not have a formal second tier but individual municipalities may add restrictions.

---

### 🗺️ North Rhine-Westphalia (Köln, Düsseldorf, Dortmund etc.)
**Category 1 (banned without exception):**
American Pit Bull Terrier, American Staffordshire Terrier, Staffordshire Bull Terrier, Bull Terrier

**Category 2 (restricted, Wesenstest can lift restrictions):**
Alano, Bullmastiff, Dogue de Bordeaux, Fila Brasileiro, Kangal, Caucasian Shepherd, Mastiff, Mastino Napoletano, Dogo Argentino, Rottweiler, Tosa Inu

NRW has one of the most detailed and extensive restricted lists in Germany. If you're moving to Cologne or Düsseldorf with a large or molosser-type breed, check carefully.

---

### 🗺️ Hesse (Frankfurt, Wiesbaden etc.)
**Category 1 (banned):**
American Pit Bull Terrier, American Staffordshire Terrier, Staffordshire Bull Terrier, Bull Terrier, Tosa Inu, Bandog

**Category 2 (regulated, can pass Wesenstest):**
Alano, American Bulldog, Bullmastiff, Dogo Argentino, Dogue de Bordeaux, Fila Brasileiro, Mastiff, Mastino Napoletano, Rottweiler

---

### 🗺️ Baden-Württemberg (Stuttgart, Mannheim, Freiburg etc.)
**No breed-specific bans at state level**, one of the more liberal states.

Instead, Baden-Württemberg uses a size + weight threshold: dogs over 40cm at the shoulder OR over 20kg require registration and liability insurance. Large dogs in public spaces must be on a leash. Aggressive behavior is addressed case-by-case, not by breed.

This makes Baden-Württemberg significantly more welcoming to large and molosser breeds.

---

### 🗺️ Lower Saxony (Hannover, Braunschweig etc.)
**Banned:**
American Pit Bull Terrier, American Staffordshire Terrier, Staffordshire Bull Terrier, Bull Terrier

**Regulated (Wesenstest required):**
Rottweiler, Doberman (both can be exempted with a passed behavioral test)

---

### 🗺️ Saxony (Dresden, Leipzig etc.)
**Banned:**
American Pit Bull Terrier, American Staffordshire Terrier, Staffordshire Bull Terrier, Bull Terrier, Tosa Inu, Dogo Argentino, Fila Brasileiro

**Regulated:**
Rottweiler, Doberman: on-leash requirement in public; can apply for exemption

---

### 🗺️ Rhineland-Palatinate (Mainz, Koblenz etc.)
**Regulated (not outright banned at state level):**
American Pit Bull Terrier, American Staffordshire Terrier, Staffordshire Bull Terrier, Bull Terrier, Rottweiler, Doberman

All regulated breeds require registration, liability insurance, and a passed *Wesenstest* for public off-leash access.

---

## The Wesenstest: How to Get an Exemption

The behavioral test (*Wesenstest* or *Charaktertest*) is Germany's mechanism for treating individual dogs as individuals rather than breed statistics. A certified assessor evaluates your dog in various real-world scenarios (around strangers, other dogs, children, loud noises, traffic) and issues a pass/fail assessment.

**What a pass gets you:**
In most states, a passed *Wesenstest* removes or significantly reduces restrictions on a Category 2 dog, including off-leash access in designated areas and freedom from mandatory muzzle requirements.

**Who can conduct it:**
Only state-approved assessors. Your vet can advise on who to contact in your area, or your district (*Ordnungsamt*) will provide a list.

**Cost:** Typically €50–150 depending on the state and assessor.

**It's worth doing.** German culture is generally very pro-dog, and officials are not looking to confiscate well-behaved animals. A passed Wesenstest is often the end of the bureaucratic story.

---

## What to Do If Your Dog Is on a Restricted List

1. **Don't panic.** Bans apply to new acquisitions in most states. Dogs already living there and registered are usually grandfathered in with permits.
2. **Register your dog immediately** with the local *Ordnungsamt* (public order office). Don't wait to be found out; proactive registration is treated far more favorably than non-compliance.
3. **Get liability insurance** (*Hundehaftpflichtversicherung*) with at least €1 million coverage. This is required for restricted breeds and a very good idea for all dogs in Germany anyway. Costs around €50–100/year.
4. **Book a Wesenstest**, even if it's not legally required in your state. It gives you documentation that your dog is well-behaved, which helps in any future dispute with landlords, neighbors, or officials.
5. **Talk to your vet.** An English-speaking vet who is familiar with the local regulations can be a real asset through this process and can often provide supporting documentation.

## A Note on Landlords

Even in states where your breed is legal, your **landlord** can refuse to allow certain dogs in a rental property, and they often do. If you're searching for a flat with a restricted breed, expect a harder search. Be upfront in your apartment search and, again, a passed *Wesenstest* certificate significantly improves your odds.
