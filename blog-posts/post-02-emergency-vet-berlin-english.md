# Emergency Vets in Berlin: 24-Hour English-Speaking Clinics

It's 11pm. Your dog just ate something off the street and won't stop retching. Or your cat is breathing oddly. Or your rabbit hasn't moved in an hour and you're scared.

This is not the moment to google in a panic, attempt to translate German vet websites, and wonder whether "Notfalldienst" means what you think it means.

So bookmark this page now, before you need it.

## What Counts as a Pet Emergency?

Not every worrying symptom needs a midnight dash across Berlin. But some absolutely do. Go immediately if your pet shows any of these:

**Go now, do not wait:**
- Difficulty breathing, choking, or blue/grey gums
- Collapse or inability to stand
- Suspected poisoning (known or suspected ingestion of toxic substances)
- Seizures
- Suspected broken bone or severe injury
- Eye injury or sudden loss of vision
- Bloated, distended, or hard abdomen (especially in large-breed dogs; this can be life-threatening)
- Suspected urinary blockage (cats straining to urinate with little or no output)
- Uncontrolled bleeding
- Loss of consciousness
- Snake bite or suspected toxic insect sting

**Can wait until morning (but call first thing):**
- Limping that isn't severe and the leg isn't visibly deformed
- Vomiting once or twice with no blood
- Mild diarrhea
- A cut that has stopped bleeding
- Ear scratching or head shaking

If you're unsure, call an emergency line and describe the symptoms. A vet tech can help you decide.

## 24-Hour Emergency Vets in Berlin

> **Note:** Emergency vet availability changes. The practices in our verified directory are regularly updated by the community, so always confirm hours when you call. The listings below represent the types of 24-hour services available in Berlin; **check the [Berlin directory](/vets/berlin) for the most current verified listings with phone numbers.**

Berlin has several dedicated 24-hour veterinary clinics (*Tiernotaufnahme* or *Tierklinik mit Notfalldienst*), concentrated mainly in the west and south of the city. Key areas to know:

**Dedicated 24-hour animal hospitals** (*Tierkliniken*): these are fully staffed around the clock with multiple vets, surgical capability, ICU units, and on-site labs. They are the best option for serious emergencies.

**Practices with Notfalldienst (emergency service)**: many regular practices participate in a rotating emergency duty system. Outside of hours, they can be reached by phone and will advise whether you need to come in. The duty vet may not be at the practice you normally visit.

**Berlin Veterinary Emergency Network:** In Berlin, emergency veterinary duty (*tierärztlicher Bereitschaftsdienst*) rotates among registered practices by district. The number to call outside of normal hours to reach the duty vet in your area is typically listed on your regular vet's answering machine. Always listen to the full voicemail; it will give you the current duty number.

## What to Say When You Call

Even at an English-speaking practice, having this information ready will get your pet seen faster:

1. **Species and breed:** "I have a 4-year-old male golden retriever"
2. **Weight:** Helpful for dosing if medication is needed immediately
3. **Symptoms and when they started:** "He started vomiting about 90 minutes ago, three times, and now he's just lying down and won't get up"
4. **Any known ingestion:** "We were at the park and I think he ate something. I'm not sure what."
5. **Vaccination and medication status:** "He's up to date on vaccinations and takes monthly flea/tick prevention"
6. **Your location:** They'll tell you if you need to come to them or if there's a closer option

**Key German phrases to understand on the phone:**
- *Kommen Sie sofort*: Come immediately
- *Das ist ein Notfall*: This is an emergency
- *Warten Sie bis morgen*: Wait until tomorrow (if they say this and you're unsure, describe the symptoms again)
- *Wir haben keinen Notfalldienst*: We don't have an emergency service (ask for the duty vet number)

## Getting There

**By car:** The fastest option if you have one. Berlin's 24-hour clinics are spread across the city, so find your nearest one before an emergency happens.

**By taxi/Uber:** Works well. Let the driver know you have a pet emergency; most will accommodate a carrier or a dog on a leash. Have the address ready.

**By U-Bahn or S-Bahn:** Dogs are allowed on all Berlin public transport at any hour with a valid ticket. Cats and small animals must be in a carrier. If your pet is seriously ill, take a taxi. You don't want to manage a sick animal on a crowded platform.

**Ambulance services for pets:** A handful of mobile vet services in Berlin will come to you for emergencies. This is slower but useful for animals that are too unstable to transport safely. Check the [Berlin directory](/vets/berlin) for mobile emergency services.

## What Emergency Vet Care Costs in Berlin

Emergency vet care in Germany is regulated by the *Gebührenordnung für Tierärzte* (GOT), but emergency surcharges apply outside of normal hours. Here's a realistic guide:

| Service | Approximate Cost |
|---------|-----------------|
| Emergency consultation (out of hours) | €80–150 |
| Blood panel / basic diagnostics | €80–200 |
| X-ray | €80–150 |
| IV fluids and hospitalisation (per night) | €150–300 |
| Surgery (emergency, e.g. foreign body removal) | €800–2,500+ |
| Intensive care (per day) | €200–500 |

**Important:** Emergency clinics in Germany typically require payment at the end of the visit, in full. Credit cards are widely accepted at larger clinics. If you have pet insurance, keep your policy number handy and ask for a detailed itemised invoice; you'll submit the claim yourself.

If cost is a concern, be upfront when you call. Veterinary social services and payment plans exist in Germany, though they vary by clinic.
