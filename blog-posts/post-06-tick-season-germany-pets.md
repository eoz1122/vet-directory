# Tick Season in Germany: Prevention & Treatment for Pets

If you moved to Germany from a country where ticks were a minor nuisance, prepare for a recalibration. Germany is one of the most tick-intensive countries in Europe — particularly in its southern and forested regions — and the diseases ticks carry here are serious business for both pets and their owners.

The good news: with the right prevention, the risk is very manageable. Here's everything you need to know.

---

## When Is Tick Season in Germany?

The short answer: March through November, with the worst of it in spring and early autumn.

Ticks in Germany (*Zecken*) become active when temperatures consistently exceed 7–8°C. In Germany's climate, that typically means:

| Period | Activity Level |
|--------|---------------|
| March–April | Season begins; first ticks emerge |
| May–June | **Peak season** — highest tick activity |
| July–August | Slightly lower activity in hot, dry spells |
| September–October | **Second peak** — activity rises again as temperatures drop |
| November–February | Largely dormant, but active on mild days above 7°C |

**The uncomfortable truth:** There is no real "off season" in Germany's milder regions. Ticks have been recorded on dogs in every month of the year in western Germany and the Rhine valley. Year-round prevention is increasingly the standard recommendation from German vets.

---

## Where Are Ticks Most Common?

Ticks don't fall from trees (a common myth). They wait in tall grass and low vegetation — a behaviour called *questing* — and attach when a warm body brushes past.

**Highest-risk areas in Germany:**
- **Bavaria and Baden-Württemberg** — particularly the Black Forest, Bavarian Forest, and foothills of the Alps. The Robert Koch Institute (RKI) designates large parts of southern Germany as FSME (tick-borne encephalitis) risk areas.
- **Hesse and Rhineland-Palatinate** — forested regions and river valleys
- **Brandenburg and Mecklenburg** — extensive woodland, popular walking areas

**Lower (but not zero) risk:**
- Urban parks in Berlin, Hamburg, Frankfurt — ticks present but lower density
- Open agricultural land with short grass

**Wherever dogs walk in Germany — whether suburban park or mountain trail — ticks are a realistic possibility.**

## The Diseases Ticks Carry in Germany

This is where Germany differs significantly from countries like the UK (which has low Lyme disease rates) or Australia (different tick species). German ticks carry several pathogens that can seriously harm your pet.

### Lyme Disease (Borreliose)
**Affects:** Dogs primarily; cats much more rarely
**Transmission:** *Borrelia burgdorferi*, carried by the common sheep tick (*Ixodes ricinus*)
**Symptoms in dogs:** Lameness (often shifting between legs), joint swelling, fever, lethargy, loss of appetite — can appear weeks after the bite
**Treatment:** Antibiotics (doxycycline); generally responds well if caught early
**Prevention:** Tick prevention products; a Lyme vaccine is available for dogs in Germany — ask your vet

### Babesiosis (Blutparasiten)
**Affects:** Dogs
**Transmission:** *Babesia canis*, carried by the ornate cow tick (*Dermacentor reticulatus*)
**Symptoms:** Sudden severe anaemia, dark urine (red/brown), weakness, high fever — can be rapidly life-threatening
**Treatment:** Specific antiparasitic drugs; requires urgent vet treatment
**Prevention:** Tick prevention is critical — Babesiosis has a high fatality rate in untreated dogs
**Note:** The Dermacentor tick is spreading north in Germany. Cases have been reported in Berlin and Hamburg that were previously rare.

### Anaplasmosis
**Affects:** Dogs and occasionally cats
**Transmission:** *Anaplasma phagocytophilum*, via *Ixodes ricinus*
**Symptoms:** Fever, joint pain, lethargy, loss of appetite, sometimes nosebleeds
**Treatment:** Antibiotics; good prognosis with early treatment

### Ehrlichiosis
**Affects:** Dogs
**Transmission:** *Ehrlichia canis*, mainly via the brown dog tick (*Rhipicephalus sanguineus*)
**Symptoms:** Fever, weight loss, bleeding disorders, swollen lymph nodes
**Note:** Less common in Germany but increasing due to travel from southern Europe

### FSME (Tick-Borne Encephalitis / Frühsommer-Meningoenzephalitis)
**Affects:** Humans primarily — **FSME does not cause significant illness in dogs or cats**. However, if your dog is finding ticks and bringing them into your home, your *own* risk of FSME exposure increases. Humans should consider FSME vaccination if living in high-risk areas — speak to your GP.

## Prevention: What Works

### Spot-On Treatments
Applied to the back of the neck every 4 weeks, spot-on treatments either repel ticks or kill them within hours of attachment (before disease transmission typically occurs). Well-known brands available in Germany:

- **Frontline** (fipronil) — widely available, repels and kills
- **Advantix** (imidacloprid + permethrin) — repels and kills; **not suitable for cats**, toxic to felines
- **Stronghold/Revolution** — also covers other parasites

**Available from:** Your vet (prescription strength), pet shops, or online pharmacies. Prescription products from your vet are typically stronger.

### Oral Preventatives (Tablets/Chews)
Increasingly popular because they're more reliably applied than spot-ons (no washing off in the rain):

- **NexGard** (afoxolaner) — monthly chew; kills ticks and fleas
- **Bravecto** (fluralaner) — lasts 12 weeks; chew or spot-on
- **Simparica** (sarolaner) — monthly chew

**These require a vet prescription in Germany.** Ask your English-speaking vet which is most appropriate for your dog's size, lifestyle, and any health conditions.

### Tick Collars
- **Seresto collar** (imidacloprid + flumethrin) — lasts up to 8 months; repels and kills ticks and fleas; available for both dogs and cats
- Effective as a supplement to other prevention; less effective as the sole method in high-exposure areas

### For Cats
Many tick products for dogs — particularly those containing **permethrin** — are **lethally toxic to cats**. Never use a dog tick product on a cat or in a space where a cat can groom it off a dog.

Safe options for cats:
- Frontline Spot-On for cats
- Seresto collar for cats
- Bravecto Spot-On for cats

Always confirm with your vet before applying any parasite prevention to a cat.

## How to Remove a Tick Correctly

Finding a tick doesn't mean disaster — Lyme disease transmission typically requires a tick to be attached for **24–48 hours**. Early removal matters.

**What you need:** A tick removal tool — either a tick tweezer (*Zeckenpinzette*) or a tick card (*Zeckenkarte*). Available at any German pharmacy (*Apotheke*) for a few euros.

**How to do it:**
1. Part the fur and locate the tick — find where it's attached to the skin
2. Grasp the tick as close to the skin as possible with the tweezer
3. Pull upward with **steady, even pressure** — do not twist or jerk
4. Remove the entire tick including the head; if the head stays in, contact your vet
5. Disinfect the bite site with antiseptic
6. Dispose of the tick (crush it in a tissue, or seal it in tape — do not flush; they survive water)

**What not to do:**
- Don't squeeze, twist, or crush the tick's body while it's still attached
- Don't use petroleum jelly, oil, nail polish, or heat — these cause the tick to regurgitate, increasing infection risk
- Don't use your bare fingers

**Keep a record:** Note the date, location on the body, and whether the tick was engorged. Watch for symptoms over the next 4–8 weeks.

## Signs Your Pet May Have a Tick-Borne Illness

Contact your vet promptly if you notice any of the following in the weeks after a tick bite — or even without a known bite during tick season:

- Sudden lameness or shifting leg pain
- Lethargy, reluctance to move
- Loss of appetite
- Fever (normal dog temperature is 38–39.2°C; higher indicates fever)
- Dark, reddish, or brown urine (emergency — possible Babesiosis)
- Swollen lymph nodes
- Unusual bruising or nosebleeds

**Dark urine after a tick bite is a medical emergency.** Go immediately.

## After-Walk Tick Checks: Making It a Habit

The single most effective non-chemical protection is a post-walk body check. Ticks take time to find a good attachment site — checking within an hour of a walk can catch many before they bite.

**Where to check on dogs:**
- Around and inside the ears
- Between the toes and under the paw pads
- Groin and inner thighs
- Under the tail and around the base of the tail
- Armpits
- Around the collar/harness area
- Eyelids (yes, really)

**For cats:** Check similarly, especially if your cat goes outdoors. Pay attention to the head and neck area where cats can't easily groom themselves.

## Tick Season Checklist

- [ ] Start tick prevention before the end of March (or year-round in mild regions)
- [ ] Confirm which prevention product is right for your pet — ask your vet
- [ ] Buy a tick tweezer and keep it in your walking bag
- [ ] Do a post-walk tick check after every walk in grassy or wooded areas
- [ ] Know the signs of tick-borne illness and act quickly
- [ ] Consider Lyme vaccination for dogs if you're in a high-exposure area (Bavaria, Hesse, forested regions)
- [ ] Humans: ask your GP about FSME vaccination if you live in or near a designated risk area
