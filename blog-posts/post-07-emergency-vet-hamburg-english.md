# Emergency Vets in Hamburg: 24-Hour English-Speaking Clinics

Hamburg at 2am. Your cat jumped from the balcony and is limping badly. Your dog ate something on the Alster path and is now retching and won't settle. Your rabbit is lying on its side and you can't tell if it's breathing normally.

Whatever it is — you need a vet, tonight, and you need one who speaks English.

Bookmark this page before you need it.

## What Counts as a Pet Emergency?

If in doubt, call. But here's a quick guide to help you decide how urgently:

**Go immediately — do not wait until morning:**
- Difficulty breathing, laboured breathing, open-mouth breathing in cats
- Collapse, inability to stand, sudden paralysis
- Suspected poisoning (any ingestion of toxic substances — chocolate, xylitol, rat poison, certain plants, medications)
- Seizures — especially if lasting more than 2–3 minutes or clustering
- Suspected fracture or serious injury
- Uncontrolled bleeding
- Sudden swollen, hard, or distended abdomen (especially large-breed dogs — this can be fatal within hours)
- Cats straining to urinate with little or no output (urinary blockage — emergency)
- Eye injury, sudden blindness, or prolapsed eye
- Loss of consciousness
- Pale, white, grey, or blue gums (sign of shock or internal bleeding)
- Dark brown or red urine (possible Babesiosis — emergency)

**Can wait until the morning clinic opens:**
- Single vomiting episode with no blood
- Mild diarrhoea, eating and drinking normally
- Minor limp, not bearing weight on one leg but no obvious injury
- Small cut or graze that has stopped bleeding
- Ear scratching or mild head shaking
- Slight lethargy without other symptoms

**If you're unsure, call an emergency number.** Describe the symptoms — a vet technician can advise you over the phone in minutes.

## Hamburg's Emergency Vet System

Hamburg operates a rotating emergency duty system (*tierärztlicher Bereitschaftsdienst*) alongside several permanent 24-hour animal hospitals. Here's how it works:

**Permanent 24-hour clinics (*Tierkliniken*):**
Hamburg has dedicated 24-hour veterinary hospitals that are fully staffed around the clock — multiple vets, surgical facilities, ICU, in-house diagnostics. These are your best option for serious emergencies. They do not require you to be an existing patient.

**Rotating duty service (*Notfalldienst*):**
Outside of hours, participating general practices take turns providing emergency cover. The current on-call number is typically listed on your regular vet's answering machine — always listen to the full message, it will give you the rotating duty number for that week.

**When you call your vet after hours:**
Listen to the full recorded message. It will either redirect you to the current duty vet, or give you the number of the nearest emergency clinic. Do not hang up after the first few seconds.

## Getting to Emergency Care in Hamburg

**By car:** The fastest option for a genuine emergency. Hamburg's 24-hour clinics are spread across the city — identify your nearest one now, before something happens.

**By U-Bahn or S-Bahn:** Dogs travel on Hamburg's public transport with a valid dog ticket (or free, in the case of Hamburg's unique rules — see our [Hamburg public transport guide](/blog/public-transport-with-dogs-hamburg-2025)). Cats and small animals must be in a carrier. For a seriously ill animal, take a taxi.

**By taxi:** Bolt, Uber, and standard Hamburg taxis generally accommodate carriers and leashed dogs. Have the destination address ready and let the driver know it's a pet emergency — most will take the fastest route without prompting.

**By ferry (HVV):** Hamburg's harbour ferries run at night and allow dogs. Useful if you're south of the Elbe heading to a clinic in the city centre or Altona.

## What to Say When You Call

Whether you're calling a 24-hour clinic or a duty vet, have this ready:

1. **Species and breed:** "I have a 6-year-old female border collie"
2. **Approximate weight:** Needed for emergency medication dosing
3. **Main symptom and when it started:** "She's been vomiting every 20 minutes since about midnight and her abdomen feels hard"
4. **Any known ingestion:** "She may have eaten something in the park earlier"
5. **Vaccination and medication history:** "She's up to date, takes a monthly flea preventative"
6. **Your location:** So they can confirm the best clinic to go to

**Useful German phrases:**
- *Mein Hund/meine Katze hat einen Notfall* — My dog/cat has an emergency
- *Ist jemand da, der Englisch spricht?* — Is there someone who speaks English?
- *Kommen Sie sofort* — Come immediately (if they say this, come immediately)
- *Warten Sie bis morgen* — Wait until tomorrow (if you're not sure, describe symptoms again)
- *Was sind Ihre Öffnungszeiten nachts?* — What are your opening hours at night?

## Emergency Vet Costs in Hamburg

Germany's vet fees are governed by the *Gebührenordnung für Tierärzte* (GOT), but out-of-hours consultations carry significant surcharges. Plan for:

| Service | Approximate Cost |
|---------|-----------------|
| Emergency consultation (evening/weekend) | €80–160 |
| Emergency consultation (overnight, 10pm–7am) | €120–200 |
| Blood work / initial diagnostics | €80–200 |
| X-ray | €80–160 |
| IV fluids and overnight hospitalisation | €150–350 per night |
| Emergency surgery (e.g. foreign body, intestinal) | €900–3,000+ |
| ICU care (per day) | €200–600 |

**Payment:** Most Hamburg emergency clinics accept Girocard (*EC-Karte*) and major credit cards. Cash is also accepted. Payment is typically required at the end of the visit, in full.

**If you have pet insurance:** Bring your policy number. Keep the full itemised invoice (*Rechnung mit Gebührenpositionen*) — you'll submit the claim yourself rather than the clinic billing your insurer directly. For more on insurance: [Pet Insurance in Germany](/blog/pet-insurance-germany-2025).

**If cost is a concern:** Be honest when you call. Hamburg has animal welfare organisations that can sometimes assist, and some clinics have social payment arrangements. Asking is always better than delaying emergency care.
