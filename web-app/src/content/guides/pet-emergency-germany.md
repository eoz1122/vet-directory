## Step 3: Call Before You Go

**ALWAYS call the emergency vet before you arrive.** This does three things:

1. **Confirms they're open and accepting emergencies** (some clinics have limited emergency hours)
2. **Prepares the vet for your arrival** (they can have equipment ready)
3. **Gives you instructions** on how to transport your pet safely

### What to Say (in English or German)

**English:**  
"Hello, I have an emergency. My [dog/cat] is [describe symptoms]. Can I bring them in now?"

**German:**  
"Hallo, ich habe einen Notfall. Mein [Hund/Katze] [symptoms]. Kann ich sofort vorbeikommen?"  
Pronounced: *HAH-loh, ikh HAH-buh EYE-nen NOHT-fall. Mine [hoond/KAHT-suh]... kahn ikh zoh-FORT for-BYE-koh-men?*

**Common symptoms in German:**

- Bleeding = blutet (BLOO-tet)
- Vomiting = erbricht sich (air-BRICHT zikh)
- Can't breathe = kann nicht atmen (kahn nikht AHT-men)
- Unconscious = bewusstlos (buh-VOOST-lohs)
- Poisoned = vergiftet (fair-GIF-tet)

## Step 4: Transport Your Pet Safely

### For Dogs

- Use a leash or carrier
- If injured, place them on a blanket and lift carefully (support the spine)
- Muzzle if necessary (injured dogs may bite)
- Keep them warm with a blanket

### For Cats

- Use a secure carrier (ALWAYSâ€”cats will bolt if scared)
- Cover the carrier with a towel to reduce stress
- Drive carefullyâ€”no sudden stops

### For Bleeding

- Apply firm pressure with a clean towel for 5+ minutes
- Don't keep checking (this disrupts clotting)

### For Poisoning

- Bring the package/container of what they ingested
- Do NOT induce vomiting unless the vet tells you to
